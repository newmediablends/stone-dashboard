### ADHD 2.0 / VAST — The Glitchy Switch

**Source:** ADHD 2.0 by Dr. Edward Hallowell and Dr. John Ratey

**VAST** = Variable Attention Stimulus Trait. The authors reframe ADHD not as a disorder of attention but as a trait — a different attentional profile. VAST captures the variability: attention surges in high-interest or high-urgency environments, drops in low-stimulation ones.

**The Glitchy Switch:**
The brain has two major networks that are normally in opposition:
- **TPN (Task Positive Network):** Engages when focused on an external task — goal-directed, outward-facing, action-oriented.
- **DMN (Default Mode Network):** Engages during rest, self-reflection, mind-wandering — inward-facing, associative.

In neurotypical brains, these networks toggle cleanly: TPN activates, DMN quiets. In VAST/ADHD brains, the switch is glitchy — both networks can fire simultaneously, or the transition is slow and unreliable. This produces:
- Starting a task but being pulled into internal reflection mid-action
- Hyperfocus in high-stimulation environments (the TPN locks in)
- Difficulty initiating when stimulation is low
- The feeling of being "stuck" even when you know what you need to do

**Why it matters for Mike:**
Understanding the glitchy switch reframes the experience — it's not laziness or weakness, it's a neural transition problem. The fix isn't willpower; it's environment design and interest engineering. High-stakes, high-interest contexts (new opportunities, meaningful work) naturally engage the TPN. Low-interest administrative tasks are the danger zone.
